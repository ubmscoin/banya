H-424: Domain time quantization: $\log(n+1)-\log(n)$

$$\Delta t_{\text{dom}}=\log(n+1)-\log(n)$$

(1) Domain time is quantized. Because system time is discrete (integer n), domain time has a discrete spacing log(n+1) - log(n).

(2) Banya formula: Δt_dom = log(n+1) - log(n). This is the domain-time interval at system time n.

(3) By Axiom 3 (screen) domain time is the rendering of system time. Since system time is discrete (integer-valued), the rendered result is also discrete.

(4) Structural consequence: in the early universe (small n), Δt_dom = log(2) - log(1) = log 2 ≈ 0.693 is large. In the late universe (large n), Δt_dom ≈ 1/n ==> 0 is small. The time interval narrows progressively.

(5) Numerics: n=1: Δt = log 2 = 0.693. n=10: Δt = log(11/10) = 0.0953. n=10⁶⁰: Δt ≈ 10⁻⁶⁰. In the present universe the domain-time interval is unobservably small.

(6) Consistency: matches H-420 (continuity sense). For large n, Δt ==> 0, so time feels continuous.

(7) Physical correspondence: time quantization is central to quantum gravity. In the Banya Framework time quantization is derived naturally from the discreteness of system time, with an interval that depends on the age of the universe.

(8) The key point is that the time quantum is variable (n-dependent), not fixed. Planck time is not a "minimum fixed time" but a "rendering resolution at the current n".

(9) Verification: confirm whether the large time intervals in the early universe (small n) leave observable traces (discrete CMB structure, discreteness of inflationary quantum fluctuations).

(10) Remaining tasks: physical derivation of the logarithmic transformation (why log), relation to spatial quantization, and reconciliation between variable time quanta and a fixed Planck time — all pending.
