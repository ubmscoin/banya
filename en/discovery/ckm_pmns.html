Value

The Standard Model has 19 free parameters — numbers the theory cannot predict and must be measured by experiment and plugged in by hand. Eight of them are mixing angles: four in the CKM matrix and four in the PMNS matrix.

The Banya Framework describes these 8 mixing angles using only 2 inputs ($\alpha$ and $2/9$). Two inputs for eight outputs leaves six independent predictions. They were not fitted — they fell out of the framework automatically.

Note on input count: the framework's underlying inputs are 3 ($\alpha$, $\alpha_s$, $2/9$). Of these, $\alpha_s$ is itself derived from $\alpha$ in the gauge-group derivation (D-03), so the count of independent inputs drops to 2 ($\alpha$, $2/9$). Depending on context we write 2 (independent) or 3 (direct usage).

In plain terms: quarks come in three generations (up/down, charm/strange, top/bottom). Leptons come in three generations (electron, muon, tau). These generations mix with each other. The angles describing how much they mix are the mixing angles. Until now, no one could explain why these angles take the specific values they do. The Banya Framework explains them.

Why It Matters

Let us unpack what this formula means.

The solar neutrino mixing angle determines how much a neutrino born in the Sun changes by the time it reaches Earth. Since Super-Kamiokande discovered neutrino oscillation in 1998 this angle has been measured with high precision, but it had never been derived from theory.

The Banya Framework expresses it as a pure mathematical constant: $3/\pi^2$. The 3 comes from the three CAS stages (R, C, S). The $\pi^2$ is the curvature of the 4-domain space. Divide one by the other and you are done. No free parameters.

More importantly, this mixing angle is linked to all the others. In the Banya Framework, once one mixing angle is derived, you re-substitute it to obtain the next. Seeds beget seeds. In the end, just 2 inputs ($\alpha$, $2/9$) describe all 8 mixing angles.

This becomes the seed for predicting the neutrino masses. Once you know the mixing angles, you can back out the mass differences, and from the mass differences you can move toward absolute masses.

Round 1. PMNS theta_12 (solar neutrino mixing angle)

Step 1. Banya formula

$$\delta^2 = (\text{time} + \text{space})^2 + (\text{observer} + \text{superposition})^2$$
4 axes, 1 operator (CAS), 3 stages (R, C, S)

The starting point is always the Banya formula.

Step 2. Three-generation substitution into the 3 CAS stages

CAS runs in three stages: R, C, S. Quarks and leptons also come in three generations. That is not a coincidence.

Each CAS stage corresponds to a particle generation. R corresponds to generation 1 (lightest), C to generation 2 (middle), and S to generation 3 (heaviest). Inter-generational mixing is the transition probability between CAS stages.

Step 3. Substitute the curvature $\pi^2$ of domain 4

The Banya Framework has 4 domains. The curvature of the phase space they generate is $\pi^2$.

Why $\pi^2$? In four-dimensional space the surface area of a sphere is $2\pi^2 r^3$. The curvature of the unit sphere is proportional to $\pi^2$. The space generated by the 4 domains has exactly this structure.

Note: here $\pi^2$ is not the scalar curvature of $S^3$ (which is 6); it is the probability-normalised topological area of $S^3$ (whose surface area is $2\pi^2$). The word "curvature" is used for intuitive convenience; strictly speaking it is a "topological area".

Step 4. Calculation

$$\sin^2(\theta_{12}) = \frac{3}{\pi^2}$$
$$= \frac{3}{9.8696}$$
$$= 0.30396$$
3 CAS stages / domain curvature

3 is the number of CAS stages. $\pi^2$ is the domain curvature. Divide 3 by $\pi^2$ and you obtain the inter-generation transition probability. That is the solar neutrino mixing angle.

Derivation: the 3 CAS stages act on a 4-dimensional phase space (4 domains). The surface area of the 4-D unit sphere $S^3$ is $2\pi^2$. If the 3 CAS stages are placed at equal intervals on this surface, the solid-angle fraction occupied by each stage is $3/(2\pi^2)$. Taking its square norm (probability) gives $\sin^2\theta_{12} = 3/\pi^2$. Why the $2$ disappears: a mixing angle is a transition probability between states. With 3 CAS stages distributed over the $S^3$ surface area $2\pi^2$, the denominator of the transition probability is the topological area of $S^3$, namely $\pi^2$ (the total surface area $2\pi^2$ probability-normalised).

Round 2. PMNS theta_23 (atmospheric neutrino mixing angle)

Derivation

$$\sin^2(\theta_{23}) = \frac{4}{7}$$
$$= \frac{\text{Swap}(4)}{\text{total}(7)}$$
$$= 0.5714$$
Observed: 0.573 -- error 0.28%

$\theta_{23}$ is the atmospheric neutrino mixing angle. It sets the probability for a muon neutrino to turn into a tau neutrino.

The Banya Framework derivation reads as follows.

  • 4 -- the number of domains in which CAS Swap succeeds. Swap operates across all 4 domains.
  • 7 -- the total degrees of freedom. Domain 4 + internal DOF 3 = 7. This is the same number that already appeared in the alpha derivation.

The fraction in which Swap succeeds is 4/7. That is the mixing probability between generations 2 and 3.

Physical meaning

The Banya Framework explains why $\theta_{23}$ sits close to 45 degrees (maximal mixing). $4/7 = 0.5714$ is slightly off $1/2$. Exact $1/2$ would mean perfect symmetry between generations 2 and 3. But CAS carries an internal DOF of 3, which breaks that perfect symmetry slightly. The amount of breaking is precisely $4/7$.

Round 3. CKM Cabibbo angle

Derivation

$$\sin(\theta_C) = \frac{2}{9}\left(1 + \frac{\pi \alpha}{2}\right)$$
$$= 0.2222 \times (1 + 0.01146)$$
$$= 0.2222 \times 1.01146$$
$$= 0.2248$$
Observed: 0.2253 -- error 0.24%

The Cabibbo angle $\theta_C$ is the largest off-diagonal element of the CKM matrix. It sets the mixing between generation 1 (up/down) and generation 2 (charm/strange) quarks.

Unpacking the derivation:

  • $2/9$ -- the Koide angle. One of the framework's three inputs. The ratio governing the mass relations among the three lepton generations.
  • $(1 + \pi\alpha/2)$ -- the first-order radiative correction. Gluon exchange among quarks slightly modifies the mixing angle.

Connection to the Koide angle

The fact that the Cabibbo angle has the Koide angle $2/9$ as its base is a very important discovery.

The Koide formula describes the mass relations of the electron-muon-tau triple. The Cabibbo angle describes inter-generational quark mixing. The fact that both start from the same number $2/9$ means the mass structure of the leptons and the mixing structure of the quarks share a common root.

This is natural within the Banya Framework: the generation structure comes from the 3 CAS stages, and both mass and mixing emerge from the same CAS.

Round 5. PMNS theta_13 (reactor neutrino mixing angle)

Direct derivation

$\theta_{13}$ is the weakest channel linking generation 1 and generation 3 neutrinos. The Daya Bay experiment in 2012 discovered that this angle is non-zero, marking a turning point in neutrino physics.

The Banya Framework derivation is as follows.

  • $2/9$ -- Compare/complete description. The Koide angle. Also the base of the Cabibbo angle.
  • $2/3$ -- the fraction of CAS stages participating in Swap (2 out of 3). Also the base of the Wolfenstein $A$ ($\sqrt{2/3}$).
  • $4/27$ -- their product. 4 = the number of Swap domains, $27 = 3^3$ = complete description cubed. Going from generation 1 to generation 3 requires traversing 3 stages, hence the cube.

Previously only an indirect derivation via the CKM-PMNS cross-relation ($\sin(\theta_C) = \frac{3}{2}\sin(\theta_{13})$) was possible. Now there is a direct formula. Inverting the cross-relation gives $\sin(\theta_{13}) = \frac{2}{3}\sin(\theta_C) = \frac{2}{3} \cdot \frac{2}{9}(1+\text{correction})$, and at zeroth order $\frac{2}{3} \cdot \frac{2}{9} = \frac{4}{27}$ matches exactly. The two routes converge on the same value.

Round 6. Refining delta_CKM

Derivation

$$\delta_{\text{CKM}} = \arctan\!\left(\frac{5}{2} + \frac{\alpha_s}{\pi}\right)$$
$$= \arctan(2.5 + 0.1183/3.14159)$$
$$= \arctan(2.5 + 0.03765)$$
$$= \arctan(2.53765)$$
$$= 1.19536 \text{ rad}$$
Observed: 1.196 rad -- error 0.053%

In the previous version the correction term was $\pi\alpha$ (the QED coupling constant). We replace it with $\alpha_s/\pi$ (the QCD coupling constant). Since this is the CP phase of the quark sector, QCD is the correct correction.

Unpacking the base $5/2$:

  • 5 -- the complete description 9 minus Swap 4. $9 - 4 = 5$. The number of non-Swap paths in CAS.
  • 2 -- the Compare stage. Comparison happens at the second of the CAS stages.
  • $5/2$ -- the path density remaining after comparison. Because CP violation arises from interference between paths, this ratio sets the size of the phase.

Why the arctan form? The CP phase is defined by interference between two paths in the complex plane. The interference phase is $\arg(z) = \arctan(\text{Im}/\text{Re})$. Here Im = non-Swap paths (5), Re = Compare stage (2), so the base $\arctan(5/2)$ is natural.

The correction $\alpha_s/\pi = 0.03778$ is a QCD one-loop correction. Gluon exchange among quarks slightly modifies the CP phase. It is larger than the QED correction ($\pi\alpha = 0.02293$), and it is closer to the experimental value.
