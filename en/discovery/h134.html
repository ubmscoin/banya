H-134: Spin-statistics = CAS atomicity $(-1)^k$

$$\psi(x_1, x_2) = (-1)^k \psi(x_2, x_1)$$

Structural correspondence

The spin-statistics theorem $\psi(x_1,x_2) = (-1)^k \psi(x_2,x_1)$ is derived from CAS atomicity.

$\psi(x_1,x_2) = (-1)^k \psi(x_2,x_1)$. $k$ = number of CAS Swap exchanges. Odd exchanges (fermions) give $(-1)$, even exchanges (bosons) give $(+1)$.

The atomicity of Swap follows from Axiom 2 (CAS sole operator). The irreversibility of the exchange follows from Axiom 5 (TOCTOU lock). A CAS Swap is atomic, so it cannot be performed halfway.

Because a CAS Swap is atomic, exchanging two particles necessarily attaches a $(-1)^k$ phase. With anticommutation ($k$ odd) Pauli exclusion (H-135) follows; with commutation ($k$ even) BEC (H-137) becomes possible.

This is a structural correspondence — an exact theorem correspondence rather than a numerical match.

Axiom 2 (CAS) and Axiom 5 (TOCTOU lock) are the basis. It forms systematic relations with H-133 (spin quantization), H-135 (Pauli exclusion), H-137 (BEC). D-40 (spin-statistics CAS) is a prior result.

The spin-statistics theorem is a foundational theorem of relativistic quantum field theory. It distinguishes Fermi-Dirac and Bose-Einstein statistics.

The standard proof (Pauli 1940) uses Lorentz invariance and microscopic causality. The Banya Framework reaches the same conclusion using only the atomicity of CAS Swap.

No violation of the spin-statistics theorem has been observed to date. If CAS atomicity is exact, violations are structurally impossible.

How anyons (fractional statistics in 2+1 dimensions) arise from CAS must be derived. The CAS interpretation of supersymmetry (fermion↔boson) is also open.
