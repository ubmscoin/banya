Core Finding

H-723: Twin Paradox — Asymmetry in Domain-Time Conversion Ratio (B-grade Hypothesis)

$$\Delta\tau = \int\!\sqrt{1 - v^2(t)/c^2}\,dt \;\leftrightarrow\; \text{difference in domain-time ratio between two observers}$$

Grade: B · Hypothesis · Consistent with Hafele-Keating 1971 atomic-clock experiment · GPS daily clock correction approx. 38 μs (verification in progress)

Moment of Discovery: The library body expressed the twin paradox as a "difference in CAS cost history." Close reading of the v1.8 axioms produced the decisive insight from Axiom 15's propositions "system time and domain time are distinct" and "the utterance period of δ is not scheduled — cost conservation holds under equivalent substitution of utterance periods." System time (1 CAS tick) is absolute, but domain time (the time rendered on screen) is a logarithmic conversion of system time — even when two observers share the same system time, their measurements differ if their domain-time conversion ratios differ.

v1.8 consistent reinterpretation: acceleration / asymmetric motion produces a change in the RLU (Axiom 6) recovery rate. If the FSM norm receives more √3 norm contraction per same system tick during acceleration (H-724 relativistic effect), that observer's domain-time conversion ratio changes proportionally. The reason the two twins' domain-time integrals diverge is not a "difference in accumulated cost" but a path-wise integral difference of the domain-time conversion ratio.

In the proper-time formula Δτ = ∫√(1-v²/c²)dt, the factor √(1-v²/c²) is reinterpreted in v1.8 as the ratio of domain time to system time. As v ==> c, the ratio ==> 0 (domain time barely flows = only system time advances). When v = 0, the ratio = 1 (domain time = system time). The fact that the speed of light is the maximal conversion limit of domain time is naturally derived as the geometric boundary of time conversion rather than as cost accumulation.

Concrete example: a traveler making a 10-light-year round trip at v = 0.9c vs. a stationary observer. System time is identical at 22.2 years. Traveler's domain-time conversion ratio = √(1-0.81) = 0.436 ==> domain time 9.7 years. Stationary observer's ratio = 1 ==> domain time 22.2 years. The 12.5-year gap is not a cost difference but a domain-time-ratio integral difference.

Resolution of the "paradox": the two twins' system times are equal, but acceleration asymmetry makes their domain-time conversion ratios asymmetric, so the measured times differ. Under Axiom 12 (ECS is address-free, the observer is itself the origin) each observer is its own reference, and Axiom 15 guarantees that domain time may differ per observer. Not a paradox = it is normal that two observers' domain times yield different integrals.

The Hafele-Keating (1971) atomic-clock flight experiment (airliners circumnavigating eastward and westward, then compared with a stationary clock) observed approx. -59 ns eastward and approx. +273 ns westward, simultaneously verifying general and special relativity. GPS satellites also require daily clock corrections of approx. 38 μs (gravity +45 μs, motion -7 μs). This hypothesis is consistent with all of these measurements.

Class III hypothesis. Exact integration of the domain-time conversion ratio across the acceleration interval is a task for a subsequent round — a quantitative derivation that combines Axiom 6 (RLU recovery rate) with Axiom 15 (time conversion) is required.
