Core Finding

H-335: Measurement strength = observer filter bandwidth

$$\text{measurement strength}\propto\text{observer filter bandwidth (bits per fire)}$$

Measurement strength is derived as observer filter bandwidth (number of bits filtered per δ utterance). Strong measurement = all 7 bits filtered; weak measurement = only 1 or 2 bits filtered.

Banya formula: measurement strength $\propto$ observer filter bandwidth (bits per fire). All 7 bits = full collapse. $k$ bits ($k < 7$) = partial collapse.

Axiomatic grounds: Axiom 10 (observer = entry point, filter) defines filter bandwidth. Axiom 15 (δ = 7 effective bits) sets the maximum bandwidth at 7. The number of domains accessed by the entry point determines the bandwidth.

Structural consequence: strong measurement (7-bit filter) collapses the quantum state fully onto an eigenstate. Weak measurement (1 to 2 bits) only partially collapses and yields a weak value. Measurement strength is not continuous but discrete (1 to 7 bits).

Numerics: 7 stages of filter bandwidth $k = 1, 2, ..., 7$. $k = 7$: full measurement, collapse probability 1. $k = 1$: minimal measurement, collapse probability $1/7$.

Consistency: H-334 (decoherence rate = translation efficiency) explains how filter bandwidth affects decoherence. H-322 (Wigner's friend) attributes the paradox to the difference in filter range between two observers.

Physical correspondence: weak measurement (Aharonov-Albert-Vaidman, 1988). A technique that continuously adjusts measurement strength to minimally disturb a quantum state. Weak values can exceed the eigenvalue range.

In conventional quantum mechanics, measurement strength is controlled by the system-meter coupling strength. In Banya, it is a discrete parameter — the filter bandwidth (bit count).

Verification: weak measurement experiments confirm strength control. It needs to be checked how the discrete bandwidth (1 to 7 bits) corresponds to continuous coupling strength.

Remaining task: clarify how the 7-stage discrete measurement strength converges in the continuous weak-measurement limit. Derive how anomalous weak values ($>$ eigenvalues) emerge from partial filtering.
