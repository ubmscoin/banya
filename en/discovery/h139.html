H-139: Spin $1/3$ forbidden = bit indivisibility

$$s \neq \frac{1}{3}, \frac{1}{5}, \ldots \quad (\text{bit indivisibility})$$

Structural correspondence

The reason spin $1/3$, $1/5$, etc., are forbidden is derived from the indivisibility of the bit.

$s \neq 1/3, 1/5, \ldots$ (bit indivisibility). A d-ring bit takes only $0$ or $1$, so partitions below the $1/2$ unit are structurally impossible.

Discreteness of the bit ($0/1$) follows from Axiom 15 (8-bit structure). The role of the SP bit is fixed by Axiom 9 (complete-descriptor induction). H-133 (spin quantization = SP bit count / 2) is the prior result.

Because the minimum unit of a bit is $1$, the minimum unit of spin is $1/2$. To get $1/3$ one would have to divide a single bit into three parts, but a bit is indivisible, so it is impossible. Therefore only $s = 0, 1/2, 1, 3/2, 2, \ldots$ exists.

This is a structural correspondence — a prohibition rule rather than a numerical error. Absence of spin $1/3$ particles is the prediction.

Axiom 9 (complete-descriptor induction) and Axiom 15 (8-bit structure) are the basis. It forms systematic relations with H-133 (spin quantization) and H-134 (spin-statistics).

No spin $1/3$ particle has ever been discovered in nature. Theoretically there is no spin $1/3$ representation in 3+1 dimensions.

Standard quantum mechanics shows from SU(2) representation theory that spin must be $n/2$ ($n$ integer). The Banya Framework offers a deeper reason: bit indivisibility.

Searches for spin $1/3$ particles are ongoing in existing particle physics experiments. Their non-discovery is itself consistent with the Banya Framework.

How the fractional spin of 2+1-dimensional anyons is permitted within the d-ring structure must be explained. This connects to the extension task of H-134 (spin-statistics).
