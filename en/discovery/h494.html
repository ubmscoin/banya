Grade: B

The pion is the pseudo-Goldstone boson of chiral symmetry breaking, and its mass is non-zero because the quark mass is finite. In the Banya framework, this is because a residual norm $\epsilon$ remains after chiral symmetry breaking (H-498) occurs during FSM norm assignment (Axiom 14). If $\epsilon = 0$ the pion is an exact Goldstone boson (mass 0); if $\epsilon > 0$ it is a pseudo-Goldstone.

Axiom 14 (FSM norm = mass), H-498 (chiral symmetry breaking = FSM norm asymmetry), Axiom 12 (FSM closure)

Axiom 14 (FSM $000 \to 111$ cycle ==> norm assignment), Axiom 12 (FSM closure ==> confinement ==> bound state), Axiom 4 (cost $+1$ ==> a non-zero residual norm is a residual cost), Axiom 2 (CAS Compare ==> quark mass difference)

Why $m_\pi \ll m_\rho$: the pion has only a residual norm, so it is much lighter than other hadrons. $m_\pi^2 \propto m_q$ (Gell-Mann-Oakes-Renner) means the residual norm scales linearly with the quark FSM norm. Why the pion mediates the nuclear force (H-496): it is the lightest bound state of FSM coupling, so the exchange cost is minimal.

$m_{\pi^\pm} = 139.57\;\text{MeV}$, $m_{\pi^0} = 134.98\;\text{MeV}$. $f_\pi = 92.1\;\text{MeV}$. Consistent with the GOR relation.

GOR relation error $< 5\%$. Lattice QCD has confirmed the $m_\pi(m_q)$ dependence.
