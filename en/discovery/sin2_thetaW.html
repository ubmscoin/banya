That is a shift of the question, not an answer.

The Banya Framework takes a different approach. It finds the path by which $\sin^2\theta_W$ is fixed directly out of the CAS cost structure. Four rounds were executed, yielding four candidates. The best candidate has an error of 0.005%. Because no unique answer has been confirmed yet, the report is marked incomplete. But securing four candidates where in 40 years nobody had produced even one is itself significant.

Round 1. 0th-order Approximation

The first round. The Banya Framework 5 steps are followed exactly. Only known constants go in. There is no previous-round output to feed back, so this is the purest starting point.

Step 1. Banya Formula

Start from the Banya formula.

$$\delta^2 = (\text{time} + \text{space})^2 + (\text{observer} + \text{superposition})^2$$
The fundamental formula of the Banya Framework. Four domains, orthogonal across two axes.

This formula is the frame that describes every change in the world. time and space are the physical background; observer and superposition are observation and superposition. There are four domains, and $\delta$ is the total amount of change.

Step 2. Norm Substitution

Substitute the delta of the Banya formula with the CAS cost structure. CAS is Compare-And-Swap. Every state change passes through the three stages Read, Compare, Swap.

$$\delta = \text{Read} + \text{Compare} + \text{Swap}$$
CAS cost decomposition. The total change $\delta$ is the sum of the costs of the three stages.

Norm substitution means replacing the abstract formula with a concrete cost structure. The four domains of the Banya formula are mapped onto the three stages of CAS. Degrees of freedom are not lost in this mapping; they are rearranged in cost space.

Step 3. Constant Substitution

$$\text{Read} = \alpha_{\text{weak}} \sim 1/30 \quad\text{(weak coupling constant)}$$
$$\text{Compare} = \alpha_{\text{em}} \sim 1/137 \quad\text{(electromagnetic coupling constant)}$$
$$\text{Swap} = \alpha_{\text{gravity}} \sim 1 \quad\text{(gravity, natural units)}$$
When CAS acts on a pair of domains the four forces appear (H-45).

The Read, Compare, and Swap stages of CAS each have a different cost structure. Read cost ~ 1/30 (weak coupling constant), Compare cost ~ 1/137 (electromagnetic coupling constant). The four forces appear as collective effects when CAS acts on a pair of domains (H-45).

Step 4. Domain Transformation

The Weinberg angle $\sin^2\theta_W$ is the mixing ratio between electromagnetism and the weak force. In the Standard Model the definition is:

$$\sin^2\theta_W = \frac{g'^{\,2}}{g^2 + g'^{\,2}}$$
$g$ = SU(2) coupling constant, $g'$ = U(1) coupling constant

Reinterpret this within the CAS cost structure. $\sin^2\theta_W$ is the share of the Compare cost inside the Read cost. Compare (electromagnetism) is contained as part of Read (weak), because electroweak unification means what was originally one thing has split.

$$\sin^2\theta_W = \frac{\text{Compare cost}}{\text{Read cost}}$$
Interpretation of the electroweak mixing angle in the CAS structure

But CAS has 3 internal degrees of freedom (Read, Compare, Swap), and the ratio of these within the full domain space must be computed. The full domain space is $4\pi$ (the solid angle of the unit sphere). Therefore:

$$\sin^2\theta_W = \frac{\text{internal DOF}}{\text{total solid angle}} = \frac{3}{4\pi}$$
CAS internal DOF 3 divided by the full solid angle $4\pi$

Step 5. Discovery

$$\sin^2\theta_W = \frac{3}{4\pi} = 0.23873$$
$$\text{Experimental value: } 0.23122$$
$$\text{Error: } 3.25\%$$
0th-order approximation. CAS internal DOF / total solid angle

3.25% is acceptable for a 0th-order approximation. It is rougher than Round 1 of the $\alpha$ derivation (0.53% error), but the direction is set. The geometric interpretation — the share of the 3 CAS internal DOF within the $4\pi$ solid angle — is the key.

Interpretation: why $3/(4\pi)$? Place three points on a sphere; this is the ratio of the region dominated by those three points relative to the full solid angle of the sphere ($4\pi$ steradian). The 3 stages of CAS are a structure that divides the spherical domain space evenly. Just as the interior angles of an equilateral triangle are 60 degrees, when the 3 CAS stages form an equilateral triangle on the sphere their share naturally becomes $3/(4\pi)$.

$$\text{CAS DoF} \;\xrightarrow{\text{curved}}\; \text{Vol}(\text{SU}(2)) = 2\pi^2$$
Curved-surface arrangement of the CAS degrees of freedom. $\text{SU}(2)$ is the 3-sphere $S^3$, whose volume is $2\pi^2$.

$\text{SU}(2)$ is the gauge group of the weak force. The volume of this group determines the actual "size" of the CAS Read stage. In Round 1 we used $4\pi$, which is the solid angle of $S^2$ (the 2-sphere). If the weak force is $\text{SU}(2)$, then using the volume $2\pi^2$ of $S^3$ (the 3-sphere) is more accurate.

$\sin^2\theta_W$ is the share along the $\text{U}(1)$ direction. We compute the share of $\text{U}(1)$ inside the full gauge space $\text{SU}(2) \times \text{U}(1)$, weighted by CAS DOF.

Result after applying the $\text{SU}(2)$ volume correction
