Round 1. CAS Mapping of the Sakharov Three Conditions

The base equation of the Banya Framework. 4 axes, 1 operator (CAS).

Step 2. The irreversible R, C, S order of CAS

CAS (Compare-And-Swap) consists of three stages.

  • R (Read) -- reads the current state
  • C (Compare) -- compares against the expected value
  • S (Swap) -- swaps in the new value if they match

This order cannot be reversed. R precedes C, C precedes S. You cannot do S first and R later. That is irreversibility.

Step 3. Substitute Sakharov's three conditions

In 1967 Sakharov showed that for matter to outnumber antimatter three conditions must hold simultaneously.

  • Condition 1 -- baryon-number non-conservation (B violation)
  • Condition 2 -- breaking of C symmetry and CP symmetry
  • Condition 3 -- departure from thermal equilibrium

If even one of these is missing, no matter survives. In the Standard Model these conditions had to be explained by separate mechanisms.

Round 2. Derivation of the Baryon-to-Photon Ratio

Round 1 confirmed that matter can survive. Round 2 calculates how much survived.

The fourth power of alpha

$$\alpha^4$$
Compare cost of each of the four domains

The Banya Framework has four domains: time, space, observer, superposition. At the Compare stage of CAS each domain pays a coupling cost of $\alpha$. Four domains each paying once gives $\alpha^4$.

Why the per-domain cost is $\alpha$: $\alpha$ is the cost of the CAS Compare stage (D-01). In baryon generation, for a matter-antimatter asymmetry to arise, a Compare (comparison judgment) must occur once in each domain. Four domains × Compare cost $\alpha$ = $\alpha^4$.

Put simply: to produce matter, all four axes must be traversed once each, and each passage costs a factor of $1/137$. Four cuts give $(1/137)^4$.

CP-violation fraction of the weak sphaleron

$$\times\;\sin^2\theta_W$$
Fraction at which the weak sphaleron violates CP

The Weinberg angle $\theta_W$ is the mixing angle between the weak and electromagnetic forces. $\sin^2\theta_W \approx 0.231$. In the sphaleron process this is exactly the fraction at which CP is violated.

Caveat: in the Standard Model the magnitude of CP violation is measured by the Jarlskog invariant $J$. The role of $\sin^2\theta_W$ here is not CP violation itself, but the electroweak mixing fraction at which the weak gauge bosons participate in the sphaleron process. This ratio controls the size of baryon-number violation. That is, $\sin^2\theta_W$ is the fraction "how many sphalerons change baryon number," not the fraction "how badly CP is broken."

Analogy: toss a coin where heads and tails do not have exactly equal probability. It is tilted by 0.231 to one side. That tilt produces the difference between matter and antimatter.

Forward-backward radiation correction

$$\times\;\left[1 - 2\!\left(4 + \frac{1}{\pi}\right)\alpha\right]$$
Correction from forward and backward radiation

Even after matter is produced, some of it collides with photons and disappears again. This term corrects for that "amount that flows back."

  • 4 -- contributions of the four domains
  • $1/\pi$ -- geometric factor of circular feedback
  • $2\alpha$ -- forward (matter creation) and backward (matter annihilation), each costing $\alpha$

This correction is very small (about 0.06). The main contribution comes from $\alpha^4 \times \sin^2\theta_W$; the correction is fine adjustment.

Round 3. Physical Interpretation

Success and failure of CAS Swap

The Swap stage of CAS yields two outcomes.

  • Swap success = matter is created
  • Swap failure = antimatter is created

Right after the Big Bang, a vast number of CAS operations executed simultaneously. Most produced matter-antimatter pairs that met and turned into light. But in a very small minority of CAS operations, Swap succeeded only on one side. That minority is today's universe.

Origin of the asymmetry

Why does it tilt to one side? Because of the R, C, S order of CAS.

At the Compare stage one bit of information is irreversibly consumed. Once you compare the read value with the expected value, that comparison result cannot be undone. This 1-bit irreversible consumption creates the tiny difference between the forward direction (matter) and the backward direction (antimatter).

Analogy: in a coin toss, the tosser's right hand is very slightly stronger than the left. The difference is almost invisible on any single throw, but after a billion throws one side ends up ahead by one. In CAS the irreversibility of Compare is exactly that "right hand."
