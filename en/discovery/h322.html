Core Finding

H-322: Wigner's friend paradox = observer filter range difference

$$\text{observer}_A\text{ filter}\neq\text{observer}_B\text{ filter};\;\delta\text{ serves both}$$

The Wigner's friend paradox is resolved as a difference in observer filter ranges. The two observers (Wigner, friend) each have different filter ranges (Axiom 10), and since δ is global it utters to both.

Banya formula: $\text{observer}_A\text{ filter} \neq \text{observer}_B\text{ filter}$, and δ utters to both. The fact that the screen outputs differ appears paradoxical.

Axiomatic basis: Axiom 10 (observer = entry point, filter) defines the per-observer filter range. Axiom 15 (δ = global) guarantees δ's simultaneous utterance on both sides.

Structural consequence: the paradox arises because the two filter outputs differ inside the FSM (screen). At the δ level there is only one equals sign, so no contradiction exists. The fact that the "friend"'s screen output and "Wigner"'s screen output differ is the natural result of a filter-range difference.

Numerics: in the Wigner's friend scenario, the ratio of the two observers' filter bandwidths determines the outcome difference. If the filter ranges are identical, the outputs are identical (the paradox vanishes).

Consistency: in H-335 (measurement strength = observer filter bandwidth), the quantification of filter range is proposed. In H-337 (illusion of free will), the structure by which an observer mistakes its own filter output for a "choice" is explained.

Physical correspondence: Wigner's friend paradox (1961). A thought experiment on the observer dependence of quantum measurement. A sharper paradox has recently been proposed in the extension (Frauchiger-Renner, 2018).

In conventional quantum mechanics, the Wigner's friend paradox is addressed via many-worlds, quantum Bayesianism (QBism), or relational interpretations. Banya provides a structural answer: a difference in observer filters.

Verification: the extended Wigner's friend experiment of Proietti et al. (2019) has been performed. The results suggest that "facts" can be inconsistent across observers, in agreement with the filter-difference interpretation.
