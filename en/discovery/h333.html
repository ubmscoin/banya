H-333: Quantum Zeno effect = Swap suppression by iterated observer-δ interaction

$$\text{frequent }\delta\to\text{observer loop}\;\Rightarrow\;\text{Swap never reached}$$

The quantum Zeno effect is derived as Swap suppression by iterated observer-δ interaction. When the observer rapidly and repeatedly receives δ utterances, the pipeline keeps restarting at the filter stage.

Banya formula: frequent δ ==> observer loop $\Rightarrow$ Swap is never reached. Pipeline: trigger ==> filter ==> (restart) ==> filter ==> ... update and render are never reached.

Axiomatic grounds: Axiom 15 (δ = global flag, utterance) governs δ-observer interaction. Axiom 10 (observer = filter) supplies the filter-restart mechanism. Axiom 4 (cost) gives Swap non-arrival = no state change.

Structural consequence: the higher the observation frequency, the more often the pipeline restarts at the filter, and the less likely Swap (state change = collapse) is reached. "Observing prevents change" = quantum Zeno effect. Observation suppresses collapse.

Numerics: the smaller the observation interval $\Delta t$, the closer the non-collapse probability $P \approx 1 - (\Gamma\Delta t)^2 \to 1$. With $N$ observations: $P_N \approx (1 - (\Gamma t/N)^2)^N \to 1$ as $N \to \infty$.

Consistency: H-338 (quantum anti-Zeno effect) explains the opposite phenomenon (observation accelerates collapse). H-335 (measurement strength = filter bandwidth) quantifies the observation strength.

Physical correspondence: quantum Zeno effect (Misra-Sudarshan, 1977). "A watched pot never boils". Experimentally confirmed in ion traps, neutron spins, etc.

In conventional quantum mechanics, the Zeno effect arises from repeated application of the projection postulate. In Banya, it is a structural mechanism of pipeline restart (filter iteration).
