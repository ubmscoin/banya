Round 1. From CAS degrees of freedom to gauge generators

Step 1. Banya formula

$$\delta^2 = (\text{time} + \text{space})^2 + (\text{observer} + \text{superposition})^2$$
Banya formula: 4-axis orthogonal structure, the minimal description of state change

The Banya formula describes every state change as an orthogonal sum over the 4 axes. The operator that executes this state change is CAS.

Step 2. Internal structure of CAS

CAS (Compare-And-Swap) consists of 3 stages. Each stage has a different internal degree of freedom.

CAS stageRoleInternal DOFMeaning
ReadReads the current state1Reading is one. It takes the state as it is. No room for choice.
CompareCompares with the expected value2Comparison is two: "equal" or "not equal". A binary verdict.
SwapExchanges conditionally4Exchange is four: the number of ways to place 2 values into 2 slots. $2 \times 2 = 4$.

The $2 \times 2 = 4$ counting for Swap, unpacked: Swap places 2 values (the current value and the new value) into 2 slots (the original location and the target location). Number of cases: $2 \times 2 = 4$. Concretely: (1) both stay, (2) only the current value moves, (3) only the new value moves, (4) both exchange.

The internal degrees of freedom of CAS are (1, 2, 4). These numbers come out of the CAS structure by necessity. No one designed them; the essence of the operation determines them.

Step 3. Substituting the gauge-group mapping hypothesis

When an operation with degree of freedom n is extended to a continuous symmetry group, what is the number of generators of that group?

  • Operation with DOF 1: $U(1)$. 1 generator. A 1-dimensional rotation has 1 axis to rotate about.
  • Operation with DOF 2: $SU(2)$. Generators $2^2 - 1 = 3$. Removing the determinant-one condition from $2 \times 2$ unitary matrices leaves 3.
  • Operation with DOF 4: this is the key.

Step 4. Domain transformation -- why 1 drops out of Swap

Swap has DOF 4. An operation with DOF 4 extended to a continuous symmetry group would seem to give $U(4)$. But it is not $U(4)$ — it becomes $SU(4-1) = SU(3)$.

$$\text{Read}(1) \longrightarrow U(1):\; \dim = 1$$
$$\text{Compare}(2) \longrightarrow SU(2):\; \dim = 2^2 - 1 = 3$$
$$\text{Swap}(4) \longrightarrow SU(4-1) = SU(3):\; \dim = 3^2 - 1 = 8$$
Mapping from CAS degrees of freedom to gauge-generator counts

Step 5. Why 1 drops out of Swap — the det=1 condition

Why is it 3 and not 4 in Swap? This is the most important point.

Swap is exchange. Before and after exchange the total norm must be preserved. "After the exchange something grew" or "after the exchange something shrank" is not allowed. This is the same as the condition that the determinant equals 1, the $\det = 1$ condition.

Imposing $\det = 1$ on $U(4)$ gives $SU(4)$, but one of Swap's 4 degrees of freedom is the "do nothing" identity. Not exchanging is not exchange. Removing it leaves $4 - 1 = 3$ substantive degrees of freedom, and the special unitary group of those is $SU(3)$.

Generator count of $SU(3)$: $3^2 - 1 = 8$. These are the 8 gluons.

$$\text{Total gauge generators}:\; 1 + 3 + 8 = 12$$
Exactly equal to the 12 gauge bosons of the Standard Model

1 photon, 3 W+, W-, Z bosons, 8 gluons. Total 12. Exactly the same as the (1, 3, 8) = 12 coming out of CAS.

Round 2. 8 gluons = CAS SU(3) adjoint representation

R, C, S as a basis

Place the 3 CAS stages Read(R), Compare(C), Swap(S) as the basis of the 3 fundamental representations of $SU(3)$. The adjoint representation of $SU(3)$ consists of the traceless $3 \times 3$ Hermitian matrices.

A $3 \times 3$ matrix has 9 components. Among them:

  • 6 off-diagonal components: transitions among R-C, R-S, C-S. Two each (real and imaginary parts), giving 3 x 2 = 6.
  • 2 traceless diagonal components: 3 diagonal entries minus the trace=0 condition leaves 2.
$$\text{off-diagonal}\; 6 + \text{traceless diagonal}\; 2 = 8$$
8 gluons = 8 Gell-Mann matrices

Round 3. Derivation of alpha_s

Derivation formula

Derive the strong coupling constant $\alpha_s$ from the electromagnetic coupling $\alpha_{\text{em}}$.

$$\alpha_s = 3 \times \alpha_{\text{em}} \times (4\pi)^{2/3}$$
The strong coupling constant arising from the CAS structure

Why this formula:

  • 3: number of CAS elements. The 3 stages Read, Compare, Swap.
  • $\alpha_{\text{em}}$: the fundamental coupling constant. The value derived from the Banya Framework in the alpha derivation report.
  • $(4\pi)^{2/3}$: domain-transformation factor. $4\pi$ is the full solid angle $4\pi$ steradians of the unit sphere ($S^2$) in 3-dimensional space, and $2/3$ reflects the fact that 2 of the 3 CAS stages (Compare and Swap) carry nontrivial degrees of freedom.

Precision check

$$\alpha_{\text{em}} = \frac{1}{137.036} = 0.007297$$
$$(4\pi)^{2/3} = (12.566)^{0.667} = 5.405$$
$$\alpha_s = 3 \times 0.007297 \times 5.405 = \mathbf{0.1183}$$

Round 4. Color confinement = CAS atomicity

CAS interpretation of baryons and mesons

A quark cannot exist alone. It exists only in combinations whose color cancels. This is called color confinement.

In CAS this corresponds to atomicity. A CAS operation cannot be broken in the middle. The entire Read-Compare-Swap is one atomic unit. Intermediate states cannot be observed from outside.

A baryon is a 3-quark system. CAS is also a 3-stage system. A baryon is stable because the 3 colors cancel; CAS is stable because all 3 stages must complete in order to commit. Same structure.

Asymptotic freedom

The first-order coefficient of the QCD beta function:

$$b_0 = \frac{11 C_A - 4 n_f T_R}{12\pi}$$
$$C_A = 3 \quad (\text{adjoint Casimir of }SU(3))$$
$$n_f = 6 \;\text{(number of quark flavors)},\quad T_R = \frac{1}{2}$$
$$b_0 = \frac{33 - 12}{12\pi} = \frac{21}{12\pi} > 0$$
Since $b_0 > 0$, asymptotic freedom holds

The numerator of $b_0$ is 21. This equals $\binom{7}{2} = 21$. It coincides with the 2-form degree of freedom of the 7-dimensional exterior algebra that appeared in the alpha57 report. This may be a coincidence, but the gauge-field DOF being 21 and the numerator of the beta function being 21 could come from the same origin. Whether the coincidence 21 = $\binom{7}{2}$ is accidental or structural remains undetermined — a future verification task.

CAS interpretation of asymptotic freedom: as energy increases (distance shortens), each CAS stage behaves more independently. As if magnifying CAS with a lens loosens the coupling between stages. Conversely, as energy decreases (distance grows), the 3 stages bunch into one and become inseparable.

Round 5. Embedding in SO(5,2)

Decomposition of 7

The alpha derivation report confirmed that the symmetric space of the Banya Framework corresponds to $SO(5,2)$. The fundamental representation of $SO(5,2)$ is 7-dimensional. Decomposing this 7-dimensional representation as $SU(3) \times U(1)$:

$$\mathbf{7} = (\mathbf{3},\,+1) + (\bar{\mathbf{3}},\,-1) + (\mathbf{1},\,0)$$
Decomposition of the 7-dim representation of $SO(5,2)$ under $SU(3) \times U(1)$
  • $(\mathbf{3},\,+1)$: fundamental representation of $SU(3)$, $U(1)$ charge $+1$. Corresponds to quarks.
  • $(\bar{\mathbf{3}},\,-1)$: anti-fundamental representation of $SU(3)$, $U(1)$ charge $-1$. Corresponds to antiquarks.
  • $(\mathbf{1},\,0)$: $SU(3)$ singlet, $U(1)$ neutral. Corresponds to leptons.

7 = 3 + 3 + 1. This means the 7-dimensional phase space of the Banya Framework naturally contains the quark-antiquark-lepton structure. The gauge group is not bolted on from outside; it emerges from the internal structure of the 7-dimensional space.
